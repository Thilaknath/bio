Google Pomelli Photoshoot: From Phone Snaps to Studio-Quality Marketing

Published: February 22, 2026

ai google gemini image-generation marketing google-labs

Google Labs just released something that's going to change how small businesses and creators approach product photography: Pomelli Photoshoot. Powered by Nano Banana (Google's latest image generation model) and backed by the intelligence of Gemini 3.1 Pro, this tool transforms a single product photo into professional marketing assets in seconds.

Pomelli Photoshoot democratizes professional product photography, what used to require a studio, photographer, and hours of post-production can now be achieved with a phone camera and an AI tool.

What is Pomelli Photoshoot?

Pomelli is Google's AI-powered marketing assistant from Google Labs. The new Photoshoot feature allows users to:

  • Generate professional product portfolios from a single reference image
  • Create multiple angles and compositions automatically
  • Produce campaign-ready banners with text overlays
  • Apply consistent, studio-quality lighting and styling

The magic happens through Nano Banana, Google's improved image generation model that offers higher accuracy, better prompt adherence, and sophisticated editing capabilities like background changes and style references.

The Technology Behind It

This release coincides with Gemini 3.1 Pro, Google's latest model that scored 77.1% on ARC AGI—above the average human baseline. Gemini 3.1 Pro brings enhanced multimodal reasoning, including the ability to analyze images through code execution (cropping, zooming, annotating) in a think-act-observe loop.

While Pomelli uses Nano Banana for image generation specifically, the broader Gemini ecosystem powers the understanding of what makes a "good" product photo and how to compose scenes that feel professional.

My Take: Disruption for SMBs and Creators

The Good

Cost savings are massive. A professional product photoshoot can cost anywhere from $500 to $5,000+ depending on the product and scope. Pomelli is free (currently rolling out in the US, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand).

Speed is unmatched. What took days of planning, shooting, and editing now takes seconds. For businesses with large product catalogs, this is transformative.

Consistency at scale. When you're selling 100 products, maintaining visual consistency across all of them is expensive. AI handles this trivially.

The Concerns

Authenticity questions. As these tools become ubiquitous, will consumers start distrusting all product photography? The "what you see is what you get" promise of photography is eroding.

Copyright gray areas. The AI is generating props, backgrounds, and styling elements. What happens when it inadvertently recreates someone else's copyrighted setup or style?

Professional disruption. Product photographers, stylists, and studios will feel this directly. The entry-level work that sustained many photographers is being automated away.

The Compliance Angle

For those of us in regulated industries, there's a governance question here too. Marketing claims need to be truthful. If your AI-generated "lifestyle shot" shows the product in a context that implies capabilities it doesn't have, you're in murky waters.

Additionally, many jurisdictions are moving toward disclosure requirements for AI-generated content. Businesses using Pomelli for marketing should be preparing for this regulatory environment.

Final Thoughts

Pomelli Photoshoot represents a genuine leap in AI capabilities for practical business use. It's not a research demo or a "coming soon" announcement, it's a working tool that produces genuinely useful output today.

For small businesses and individual creators, this removes a significant barrier to professional presentation. For existing photography professionals, it's a signal to move up the value chain toward work that requires genuine human creativity, not just technical execution.

As with most AI tools, the question isn't whether to use them, but how to use them responsibly. The technology is here. The governance frameworks are still catching up.
